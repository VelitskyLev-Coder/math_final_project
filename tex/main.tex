\section{Beverton-Holt model}
Let $N_t \geq 0$ denote the population at time $t$. We use the
scaled Beverton-Holt model
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:bh-map}
    N_{t+1} = F(N_t)
            = \frac{K r N_t}{K + N_t},
\end{equation}
where $r \geq 0$ is the growth parameter and $K>0$ is the
population-scale parameter.
\subsection{Equilibria}
An equilibrium $N^*$ satisfies $F(N^*)=N^*$. Using \eqref{eq:bh-map},
this means
\begin{equation*}
    N^* = \frac{K r N^*}{K + N^*}.
\end{equation*}
One solution is the extinction equilibrium
\begin{equation*}
    N^*_0=0.
\end{equation*}
For a positive equilibrium, $N^*>0$, we divide by $N^*$ and obtain
\begin{align*}
    1 &= \frac{K r}{K+N^*},\\
    K+N^* &= Kr,\\
    N^* &= K(r-1).
\end{align*}
The positive equilibrium exists only when $r>1$ and the population can
not be negative. Thus,
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        r\leq 1: & N^*=0 \text{ is the only equilibrium},\\
        r>1: & N^*=0 \text{ and } N^*=K(r-1) \text{ are equilibria}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
\subsection{Stability}
The derivative of the population map \eqref{eq:bh-map} is
\begin{equation*}
    F'(N)=\frac{K^2r}{(K+N)^2}.
\end{equation*}
At the extinction equilibrium,
\begin{equation*}
    F'(0)=r.
\end{equation*}
This derivative measures the growth of a very small population. Thus,
the extinction criterion for the model without fishing is
\begin{equation*}
    r \leq 1.
\end{equation*}
When $0\leq r<1$, extinction is locally stable. When $r>1$, extinction
is unstable and a positive population can persist. The case $r=1$ is
the threshold case.
At the positive equilibrium,
\begin{equation*}
    F'\bigl(K(r-1)\bigr)=\frac{1}{r}.
\end{equation*}
Consequently, whenever the positive equilibrium exists, $r>1$, it is
locally stable.
\section{Introduction of Fishing Effort}
We now introduce a fishing effort parameter $e$, where
\begin{equation*}
    0 \leq e \leq 1.
\end{equation*}
The value $e=0$ means no fishing, while $e=1$ means complete removal
after reproduction. With fishing, the one-population model becomes
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:fishing-map}
    N_{t+1}
    = F_e(N_t)
    = (1-e)\frac{K r N_t}{K+N_t}.
\end{equation}
\subsection{Equilibria}
An equilibrium satisfies $F_e(N^*)=N^*$. Using \eqref{eq:fishing-map},
this gives
\begin{equation*}
    N^* = (1-e)\frac{K r N^*}{K+N^*}.
\end{equation*}
Again, $N^*=0$ is always an equilibrium. For a positive equilibrium,
$N^*>0$, we divide by $N^*$ and obtain
\begin{align*}
    1 &= (1-e)\frac{K r}{K+N^*},\\
    K+N^* &= K r(1-e),\\
    N^* &= K\bigl(r(1-e)-1\bigr).
\end{align*}
Therefore, the positive equilibrium exists only when
\begin{equation*}
    r(1-e)>1.
\end{equation*}
Equivalently, if $r>1$, persistence requires
\begin{equation*}
    e < 1-\frac{1}{r}.
\end{equation*}
If this condition fails, then extinction is the only nonnegative
equilibrium.
The derivative of the fishing map \eqref{eq:fishing-map} is
\begin{equation*}
    F_e'(N)=\frac{K^2r(1-e)}{(K+N)^2}.
\end{equation*}
At extinction,
\begin{equation*}
    F_e'(0)=r(1-e).
\end{equation*}
Therefore, the extinction criterion with fishing is
\begin{equation*}
    r(1-e)\leq 1.
\end{equation*}
Equivalently, when $r>1$, extinction occurs when
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:fishing-extinction-effort}
    e \geq 1-\frac{1}{r}.
\end{equation}
When $r(1-e)<1$, extinction is locally stable. When $r(1-e)>1$,
extinction is unstable and the positive equilibrium exists.
At the positive equilibrium,
\begin{equation*}
    F_e'\bigl(K(r(1-e)-1)\bigr)=\frac{1}{r(1-e)}.
\end{equation*}
Whenever the positive equilibrium exists, $r(1-e)>1$, this derivative
is less than $1$, so the positive equilibrium is locally stable.

\subsection{Maximizing Fishing Yield}
We define the equilibrium fishing yield as the amount removed by fishing
at equilibrium. Since fishing removes the fraction $e$ after
reproduction, the yield is
\begin{equation*}
    Y(e)=eF(N^*).
\end{equation*}
At equilibrium, $N^*=(1-e)F(N^*)$, and therefore
\begin{equation*}
    F(N^*)=\frac{N^*}{1-e}.
\end{equation*}
Thus, for a positive equilibrium,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:yield-expression}
    \begin{aligned}
        Y(e)
            &= \frac{eN^*}{1-e}\\
            &= \frac{eK(r(1-e)-1)}{1-e}\\
            &= Ke\left(r-\frac{1}{1-e}\right).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The yield expression \eqref{eq:yield-expression} is meaningful only
below the extinction threshold in \eqref{eq:fishing-extinction-effort}.
To maximize yield, differentiate:
\begin{equation*}
    Y'(e)=K\left(r-\frac{1}{(1-e)^2}\right).
\end{equation*}
The critical point satisfies
\begin{equation*}
    r=\frac{1}{(1-e)^2}.
\end{equation*}
Therefore, for $r>1$, the yield-maximizing effort is
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:yield-optimal-effort}
    e^*=1-\frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}.
\end{equation}
For $r=1.4$ and $K=1$, \eqref{eq:yield-optimal-effort} gives
$e^*\approx 0.155$, while the extinction threshold from
\eqref{eq:fishing-extinction-effort} is $e_c\approx 0.286$.

\section{Linked Two-Population Model}
We now introduce two linked populations, denoted by $A$ and $B$. The
population sizes at time $t$ are $N_A(t)$ and $N_B(t)$. Population $A$
has parameters $r_A$ and $K_A$, while population $B$ has parameters
$r_B$ and $K_B$.
The local Beverton-Holt growth functions are
\begin{equation*}
    F_A(N_A)=\frac{K_A r_A N_A}{K_A+N_A},
    \qquad
    F_B(N_B)=\frac{K_B r_B N_B}{K_B+N_B}.
\end{equation*}
The parameter $m$ controls symmetric movement between the two
populations. A fraction $m$ of the post-growth population moves to the
other population, while a fraction $1-m$ remains in the same population.
Thus, for $0\leq m\leq 1$, the linked model without fishing is
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-model}
    \begin{aligned}
        N_A(t+1)
            &= (1-m)\frac{K_A r_A N_A(t)}{K_A+N_A(t)}
               + m\frac{K_B r_B N_B(t)}{K_B+N_B(t)},\\
        N_B(t+1)
            &= m\frac{K_A r_A N_A(t)}{K_A+N_A(t)}
               + (1-m)\frac{K_B r_B N_B(t)}{K_B+N_B(t)}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
When $m=0$, the two populations are independent. When $m=1$, the two
post-growth populations exchange locations completely at each time step.
\subsection{Equilibria}
An equilibrium of the linked model is a pair $(N_A^*,N_B^*)$ satisfying
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        N_A^*
            &= (1-m)\frac{K_A r_A N_A^*}{K_A+N_A^*}
               + m\frac{K_B r_B N_B^*}{K_B+N_B^*},\\
        N_B^*
            &= m\frac{K_A r_A N_A^*}{K_A+N_A^*}
               + (1-m)\frac{K_B r_B N_B^*}{K_B+N_B^*}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
The extinction equilibrium
\begin{equation*}
    (N_A^*,N_B^*)=(0,0)
\end{equation*}
always exists.
For $m=0$, the two populations are independent, so the positive
equilibrium is simply
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=K_A(r_A-1),
    \qquad
    N_B^*=K_B(r_B-1),
\end{equation*}
when $r_A>1$ and $r_B>1$.

A natural question is whether connecting two populations helps or hurts
the total equilibrium population. One might expect movement to only
redistribute the same total population between the two locations.
However, movement can change the total equilibrium size.

\subsection{Exact Algebraic Form}
For general $m$, the two equilibrium equations are coupled. After
clearing denominators and eliminating $N_B^*$, the nonzero equilibria are
obtained exactly from a cubic polynomial
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-equilibrium-cubic}
    P(N_A^*)=0.
\end{equation}
Once a root $N_A^*$ of \eqref{eq:linked-equilibrium-cubic} is chosen,
the corresponding value of $N_B^*$ is
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-equilibrium-nb-from-na}
    N_B^*
    =
    \frac{
        K_B N_A^*
        \left(K_A m r_A - K_A r_A + K_A + N_A^*\right)
    }{
        K_AK_Bmr_B
        -K_A N_A^*mr_A
        +K_A N_A^*r_A
        -K_A N_A^*
        +K_B N_A^*mr_B
        -(N_A^*)^2
    }.
\end{equation}
Writing
$$
    P(N_A)=a_3N_A^3+a_2N_A^2+a_1N_A+a_0,
$$
the coefficients are
\begin{align*}
    a_3 &= m-1,\\
    a_2
        &= K_A m^2r_A -4K_A mr_A +2K_A m +2K_A r_A -2K_A\\
        &\qquad{} -K_Bm^2r_B +K_Bmr_B-K_Bm,\\
    a_1
        &= -2K_A^2m^2r_A^2+K_A^2m^2r_A+3K_A^2mr_A^2
           -4K_A^2mr_A+K_A^2m\\
        &\qquad{} -K_A^2r_A^2+2K_A^2r_A-K_A^2
           +2K_AK_Bm^2r_Ar_B\\
        &\qquad{} -K_AK_Bm^2r_A-2K_AK_Bm^2r_B
           -K_AK_Bmr_Ar_B\\
        &\qquad{} +K_AK_Bmr_A+2K_AK_Bmr_B-2K_AK_Bm,\\
    a_0
        &= 2K_A^2K_Bm^2r_Ar_B-K_A^2K_Bm^2r_A
           -K_A^2K_Bm^2r_B\\
        &\qquad{} -K_A^2K_Bmr_Ar_B+K_A^2K_Bmr_A
           +K_A^2K_Bmr_B-K_A^2K_Bm.
\end{align*}
Because \eqref{eq:linked-equilibrium-cubic} is cubic, its roots have a
closed form. In practice, the cubic formula is too long to be useful, so
we treat \eqref{eq:linked-equilibrium-cubic} and
\eqref{eq:linked-equilibrium-nb-from-na} as the exact algebraic
description. The biologically meaningful equilibrium is the root giving
$N_A^*>0$ and $N_B^*>0$.
\subsection{Extinction Without Fishing}
To study extinction, we linearize the linked model \eqref{eq:linked-model}
at the extinction equilibrium $(0,0)$. For population $A$, the local
growth function is
\begin{equation*}
    F_A(N_A)=\frac{K_A r_A N_A}{K_A+N_A}.
\end{equation*}
Its derivative is
\begin{equation*}
    F_A'(N_A)=\frac{K_A^2 r_A}{(K_A+N_A)^2},
\end{equation*}
and therefore
\begin{equation*}
    F_A'(0)=r_A.
\end{equation*}
Similarly,
\begin{equation*}
    F_B'(0)=r_B.
\end{equation*}
Near extinction, both populations are small, so the first-order Taylor
approximations are
\begin{equation*}
    F_A(N_A)\approx r_A N_A,
    \qquad
    F_B(N_B)\approx r_B N_B.
\end{equation*}
Substituting these approximations into the linked model
\eqref{eq:linked-model} gives
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        N_A(t+1)
            &\approx (1-m)r_A N_A(t)+mr_BN_B(t),\\
        N_B(t+1)
            &\approx mr_A N_A(t)+(1-m)r_BN_B(t).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Thus, the scale parameters $K_A$ and $K_B$ do not appear in the
linearized extinction dynamics. In matrix form, the linearized system is
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        N_A(t+1)\\
        N_B(t+1)
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    M
    \begin{pmatrix}
        N_A(t)\\
        N_B(t)
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-linear-matrix}
    M=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        r_A(1-m) & r_Bm\\
        r_Am & r_B(1-m)
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The extinction equilibrium is locally stable when the spectral radius
of the matrix in \eqref{eq:linked-linear-matrix} satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-spectral-criterion}
    \rho(M)<1.
\end{equation}
Because $M$ is nonnegative, its spectral radius is the larger eigenvalue.
For the matrix in \eqref{eq:linked-linear-matrix}, this gives the closed
form
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-spectral-radius-closed-form}
    \rho(M)
    =
    \frac{
        (1-m)(r_A+r_B)
        +
        \sqrt{(1-m)^2(r_A-r_B)^2+4m^2r_Ar_B}
    }{2}.
\end{equation}
Thus extinction without fishing occurs exactly when
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-extinction-closed-form}
    (1-m)(r_A+r_B)
    +
    \sqrt{(1-m)^2(r_A-r_B)^2+4m^2r_Ar_B}
    <2.
\end{equation}
This recovers the independent-population condition $r_A<1$ and $r_B<1$
when $m=0$. When $m=1$, the condition becomes $r_Ar_B<1$.

\section{Linked Two-Population Model with Fishing Effort}
We now add fishing to the linked two-population model. The effort
$e_A$ is applied to population $A$, and $e_B$ is applied to population
$B$, after local growth and movement. Thus, for $0\leq e_A,e_B\leq1$,
the model becomes
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-model}
    \begin{aligned}
        N_A(t+1)
            &= (1-e_A)
            \left(
                (1-m)\frac{K_A r_A N_A(t)}{K_A+N_A(t)}
                + m\frac{K_B r_B N_B(t)}{K_B+N_B(t)}
            \right),\\
        N_B(t+1)
            &= (1-e_B)
            \left(
                m\frac{K_A r_A N_A(t)}{K_A+N_A(t)}
                + (1-m)\frac{K_B r_B N_B(t)}{K_B+N_B(t)}
            \right).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
When $e_A=e_B=0$, this reduces to the linked model
\eqref{eq:linked-model}.
\subsection{Equilibria}
An equilibrium of the linked fishing model is a pair
$(N_A^*,N_B^*)$ satisfying
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-equilibrium}
    \begin{aligned}
        N_A^*
            &= (1-e_A)
            \left(
                (1-m)\frac{K_A r_A N_A^*}{K_A+N_A^*}
                +m\frac{K_B r_B N_B^*}{K_B+N_B^*}
            \right),\\
        N_B^*
            &= (1-e_B)
            \left(
                m\frac{K_A r_A N_A^*}{K_A+N_A^*}
                +(1-m)\frac{K_B r_B N_B^*}{K_B+N_B^*}
            \right).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The extinction equilibrium
\begin{equation*}
    (N_A^*,N_B^*)=(0,0)
\end{equation*}
always exists.

When $m=0$, the two populations are independent and fishing simply
reduces the effective growth rates. The positive equilibrium is
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=K_A(r_A(1-e_A)-1),
    \qquad
    N_B^*=K_B(r_B(1-e_B)-1),
\end{equation*}
provided
\begin{equation*}
    r_A(1-e_A)>1,
    \qquad
    r_B(1-e_B)>1.
\end{equation*}

For general $m$, the equilibrium equations are still coupled. Solving
the first equation in \eqref{eq:linked-fishing-equilibrium} for
$N_B^*$ gives
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-nb-from-na}
    N_B^*
    =
    \frac{
        K_BN_A^*
        \left(K_A(r_A(1-e_A)(1-m)-1)-N_A^*\right)
    }{
        (N_A^*)^2
        +K_AN_A^*\left(1-r_A(1-e_A)(1-m)\right)
        -K_Bmr_B(1-e_A)(K_A+N_A^*)
    }.
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:linked-fishing-nb-from-na} into the second
equilibrium equation gives a cubic equation for $N_A^*$, similar to the
no-fishing cubic in \eqref{eq:linked-equilibrium-cubic}. Thus fishing
changes the coefficients of the algebraic equation, but it does not
increase the degree of the equilibrium problem.
\subsection{Extinction Criterion}
To study extinction with fishing, we linearize
\eqref{eq:linked-fishing-model} at $(0,0)$. The linearized matrix is
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-linear-matrix}
    M_f=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        r_A(1-m)(1-e_A) & r_Bm(1-e_A)\\
        r_Am(1-e_B) & r_B(1-m)(1-e_B)
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
As before, the parameters $K_A$ and $K_B$ cancel from the linearized
extinction dynamics. Extinction is determined by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-spectral-criterion}
    \rho(M_f)<1.
\end{equation}
The boundary between extinction and persistence is given by
\begin{equation*}
    \det(I-M_f)=0.
\end{equation*}
For this matrix,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-det-boundary}
    \det(I-M_f)=(Ce_A+D)e_B-(Ae_A+B),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        A &= -r_A + mr_A + r_Ar_B - 2mr_Ar_B,\\
        B &= -1 + r_A + r_B - mr_A - mr_B - r_Ar_B + 2mr_Ar_B,\\
        C &= r_Ar_B - 2mr_Ar_B,\\
        D &= r_B - mr_B - r_Ar_B + 2mr_Br_A.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
The threshold curve is obtained from the undivided boundary
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-det-condition}
    (Ce_A+D)e_B-(Ae_A+B)\geq0.
\end{equation}
For a fixed effort $e_A$, the threshold form
$e_B\geq(Ae_A+B)/(Ce_A+D)$ is meaningful only on the branch where
$Ce_A+D>0$. To make the criterion valid on the whole unit square
$0\leq e_A,e_B\leq1$, we write both parts:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-eb-threshold}
    e_B\geq\frac{Ae_A+B}{Ce_A+D}
    \qquad\text{and}\qquad
    Ce_A+D>0.
\end{equation}
The second condition does not describe another extinction mechanism. It
only restricts the threshold inequality to the side of the vertical line
$Ce_A+D=0$ where division by $Ce_A+D$ gives the correct extinction
branch. This gives the expected direction: larger fishing effort $e_B$
pushes the system toward extinction. The final extinction region is the
set of efforts for which the spectral condition
\eqref{eq:linked-fishing-spectral-criterion} holds.
The sign of $Ce_A+D$ can be understood directly from
\begin{equation*}
    Ce_A+D
    =
    r_B\left(1-m-r_A(1-2m)(1-e_A)\right).
\end{equation*}
If $1/2\leq m<1$, then $1-m>0$ and $1-2m\leq0$. Since
$r_A>0$ and $1-e_A\geq0$ on the unit square, the term
$-r_A(1-2m)(1-e_A)$ is nonnegative. Therefore
\begin{equation*}
    1-m-r_A(1-2m)(1-e_A)>0,
\end{equation*}
and hence $Ce_A+D>0$ for every $0\leq e_A\leq1$. In this case the
branch condition is automatic on the whole unit square.

If $0\leq m<1/2$, the vertical line $Ce_A+D=0$ can enter the unit square.
In this case we need the second condition in
\eqref{eq:linked-fishing-eb-threshold}. To see why, suppose instead that
$Ce_A+D\leq0$. Then
\begin{equation*}
    r_A(1-e_A)\geq\frac{1-m}{1-2m}.
\end{equation*}
From \eqref{eq:linked-fishing-linear-matrix}, the first diagonal entry of
$M_f$ is
\begin{equation*}
    r_A(1-m)(1-e_A),
\end{equation*}
and therefore
\begin{equation*}
    r_A(1-m)(1-e_A)
    \geq
    \frac{(1-m)^2}{1-2m}
    =
    1+\frac{m^2}{1-2m}
    \geq1.
\end{equation*}
Since $M_f$ is a nonnegative matrix, its spectral radius is at least as
large as any diagonal entry. Hence $\rho(M_f)\geq1$, so extinction
cannot occur when $Ce_A+D\leq0$. Thus, for $0\leq m<1/2$, the condition
$Ce_A+D>0$ is not just a technical detail of the division; it removes a
part of the unit square where extinction is impossible.

\subsection{Maximizing Fishing Yield}
We now ask how to choose the fishing efforts $e_A$ and $e_B$ in order to
maximize total equilibrium fishing yield. In this problem the parameters
$r_A$, $r_B$, $K_A$, $K_B$, and $m$ are fixed. The only control variables
are $e_A$ and $e_B$.

The equilibrium populations are
determined by the equilibrium equations of
\eqref{eq:linked-fishing-model}. For compact notation, define the
post-growth, post-movement populations before fishing by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-growth-before-harvest}
    \begin{aligned}
        G_A(N_A,N_B)
            &=
            (1-m)\frac{K_A r_A N_A}{K_A+N_A}
            +m\frac{K_B r_B N_B}{K_B+N_B},\\
        G_B(N_A,N_B)
            &=
            m\frac{K_A r_A N_A}{K_A+N_A}
            +(1-m)\frac{K_B r_B N_B}{K_B+N_B}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
At equilibrium,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-yield-constraints}
    \begin{aligned}
        N_A^*-(1-e_A)G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*) &= 0,\\
        N_B^*-(1-e_B)G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*) &= 0.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The amount harvested from population $A$ is $e_A G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)$,
and similarly for population $B$. Therefore the total equilibrium yield
is
\begin{equation*}
    Y=e_A G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)+e_B G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
Using the equilibrium constraints in
\eqref{eq:linked-fishing-yield-constraints}, this can also be written as
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-yield}
    Y
    =
    \frac{e_A N_A^*}{1-e_A}
    +
    \frac{e_B N_B^*}{1-e_B},
    \qquad
    0\leq e_A,e_B<1.
\end{equation}
Thus the yield maximization problem is constrained: changing $e_A$ and
$e_B$ also changes the equilibrium populations $N_A^*$ and $N_B^*$.
Throughout this section we restrict the fishing efforts to the unit
square $0\leq e_A,e_B\leq1$. On this square, nonnegative populations are
mapped to nonnegative populations, since $F_A,F_B\geq0$ and
$1-e_A,1-e_B\geq0$. Thus the effort constraints are the main feasibility
checks for the candidate formulas. When a boundary candidate is defined
implicitly by equilibrium equations, we choose the nonnegative
equilibrium solution.

Before comparing yield candidates, we first check the extinction
criterion \eqref{eq:linked-fishing-spectral-criterion}, or equivalently
the threshold form \eqref{eq:linked-fishing-eb-threshold}. If the
efforts lie in the extinction region, then the equilibrium population is
zero and the equilibrium yield is zero. Such efforts cannot give a
positive-yield maximum, so the candidate search below is carried out in
the persistence region.

If $e_A=e_B=0$, there is no fishing and $Y=0$. If $e_A=e_B=1$, both
post-fishing populations are zero at equilibrium and again $Y=0$.
Neither corner can be a positive-yield maximum, so these two zero-yield
corners are omitted from the candidate table.

The remaining part of the square $0\leq e_A,e_B\leq1$ is split into
boundary cases and the open interior. Some cases are symmetric, so we
treat them together.

\subsubsection{Complete-Harvest Edges}
The complete-harvest edges are $e_A=1$ and $e_B=1$. They are boundary
cases, but they are not automatically zero-yield cases. We start with
the edge $e_A=1$, where patch $A$ is completely harvested. Then
$N_A^*=0$. If $e_B<1$, the remaining equilibrium equation is
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*
    =
    (1-e_B)(1-m)
    \frac{K_Br_BN_B^*}{K_B+N_B^*}.
\end{equation*}
For a positive remaining population, divide by $N_B^*$:
\begin{align*}
    1
        &=
        (1-e_B)(1-m)
        \frac{K_Br_B}{K_B+N_B^*},\\
    K_B+N_B^*
        &=
        K_Br_B(1-m)(1-e_B).
\end{align*}
Thus, on this edge,
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*=K_B\left(r_B(1-m)(1-e_B)-1\right),
\end{equation*}
whenever this expression is positive.

Now compute the yield on the same edge. Since $N_A^*=0$, the growth in
patch $A$ is zero. Hence
\begin{equation*}
    G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)=mF_B(N_B^*),
    \qquad
    G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*)=(1-m)F_B(N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
Because $e_A=1$, the total yield is
\begin{equation*}
    Y=
    G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)+e_BG_B(N_A^*,N_B^*)
    =
    \left(m+(1-m)e_B\right)
    F_B(N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
To make this a one-variable expression, write
\begin{equation*}
    q=1-e_B,
    \qquad
    c=1-m.
\end{equation*}
Then
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*=K_B(r_Bcq-1),
    \qquad
    F_B(N_B^*)=\frac{N_B^*}{cq}.
\end{equation*}
The yield becomes
\begin{align*}
    Y(q)
        &=
        \left(1-cq\right)
        \frac{K_B(r_Bcq-1)}{cq}\\
        &=
        K_B\left(r_B+1-\frac{1}{cq}-r_Bcq\right).
\end{align*}
Therefore
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dY}{dq}
    =
    K_B\left(\frac{1}{cq^2}-r_Bc\right).
\end{equation*}
The critical point satisfies
\begin{equation*}
    q=\frac{1}{(1-m)\sqrt{r_B}}.
\end{equation*}
Since $q=1-e_B$, the complete-harvest-in-$A$ candidate is
\begin{equation*}
    e_B^*=1-\frac{1}{(1-m)\sqrt{r_B}},
\end{equation*}
and the corresponding candidate yield is
\begin{equation*}
    Y=K_B(\sqrt{r_B}-1)^2.
\end{equation*}

The edge $e_B=1$ is symmetric. In that case $N_B^*=0$, patch $B$ is
completely harvested, and the same calculation with the roles of $A$
and $B$ exchanged gives
\begin{equation*}
    e_A^*=1-\frac{1}{(1-m)\sqrt{r_A}}.
\end{equation*}
The corresponding candidate yield is
\begin{equation*}
    Y=K_A(\sqrt{r_A}-1)^2.
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{No-Take Edges}
The no-take edges are $e_A=0$ and $e_B=0$. These are different from the
complete-harvest edges: here one patch is protected from fishing, while
the other patch may still be harvested. Direct symbolic elimination in
the population variables gives a degree-six polynomial, so the result is
not pleasant to use in that form. A cleaner form is obtained by changing
variables from the equilibrium populations to the local growth
derivatives
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        a&=\sqrt{r_A},
        &
        b&=\sqrt{r_B},\\
        x&=\sqrt{q_A},
        &
        y&=\sqrt{q_B},\\
        q_A&=F_A'(N_A^*),
        &
        q_B&=F_B'(N_B^*).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
The quantities $q_A$ and $q_B$ are the marginal, or local, growth
derivatives at the equilibrium populations. They measure the local slope
of the Beverton-Holt growth functions at the candidate equilibrium.

For the Beverton-Holt map,
\begin{equation*}
    F_A'(N_A)=\frac{K_A^2r_A}{(K_A+N_A)^2},
    \qquad
    F_B'(N_B)=\frac{K_B^2r_B}{(K_B+N_B)^2}.
\end{equation*}
Using $q_A=F_A'(N_A^*)$, $x=\sqrt{q_A}$, and $a=\sqrt{r_A}$ gives
\begin{equation*}
    x=\frac{K_Aa}{K_A+N_A^*}.
\end{equation*}
Solving this equation for $N_A^*$ gives
$N_A^*=K_A(a-x)/x$. The same calculation in patch $B$ gives the
corresponding expression for $N_B^*$. Thus this change of variables gives
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:no-take-population-recovery}
    N_A^*=K_A\frac{a-x}{x},
    \qquad
    N_B^*=K_B\frac{b-y}{y},
\end{equation}
Substituting these expressions back into the Beverton-Holt growth
functions gives
\begin{equation*}
    F_A(N_A^*)=K_Aa(a-x),
    \qquad
    F_B(N_B^*)=K_Bb(b-y).
\end{equation*}
Now consider the no-take edge $e_A=0$. On this edge, the yield is
$Y=e_BG_B(N_A^*,N_B^*)$, and the equilibrium constraints are
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*),
    \qquad
    N_B^*=(1-e_B)G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
Introduce Lagrange multipliers for these two constraints. On this edge
the variables are $N_A$, $N_B$, and $e_B$, so we use the Lagrangian
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
    \mathcal{L}
    ={}&e_BG_B(N_A,N_B)
    +\lambda_A\left(N_A-G_A(N_A,N_B)\right)\\
    &+\lambda_B\left(N_B-(1-e_B)G_B(N_A,N_B)\right).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
All derivatives below are evaluated at the candidate equilibrium. The
derivative with respect to $e_B$ is
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial e_B}
    =
    G_B+\lambda_BG_B
    =
    (1+\lambda_B)G_B.
\end{equation*}
For a positive-yield candidate, $G_B>0$, so this gives
$\lambda_B=-1$. The derivative with respect to $N_A$ then gives
\begin{equation*}
    \lambda_A
    =
    -\frac{m q_A}{1-(1-m)q_A}.
\end{equation*}
Finally, the derivative with respect to $N_B$ gives
\begin{equation*}
    (1-m)q_B-mq_B\lambda_A-1=0.
\end{equation*}
Substituting the expression for $\lambda_A$ into this last equation and
clearing the denominator gives
\begin{equation*}
    (1-m)(q_A+q_B)+(2m-1)q_Aq_B=1.
\end{equation*}
Since $q_A=x^2$ and $q_B=y^2$, the no-take stationarity condition becomes
the single relation
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:no-take-stationarity-curve}
    (1-m)(x^2+y^2)+(2m-1)x^2y^2=1.
\end{equation}
This is the curve shown in orange in Figure~\ref{fig:no-take-yield-examples}. It is the set of effort pairs for
which the no-take first-order condition is satisfied after changing from
population variables to local growth-derivative variables.

Equation \eqref{eq:no-take-stationarity-curve} can be solved explicitly
for one derivative variable in terms of the other. To work on the edge
$e_A=0$, solving it for $y$ gives
\begin{equation*}
    y_A(x)
    =
    \sqrt{
        \frac{1-(1-m)x^2}
             {1-m+(2m-1)x^2}
    }.
\end{equation*}
This only says which pairs $(x,y)$ satisfy the no-take stationarity
condition. It does not yet tell us which value of $x$ gives an actual
equilibrium. Since $e_A=0$, patch $A$ is not harvested, so its
equilibrium equation is
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=(1-m)F_A(N_A^*)+mF_B(N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
Now use the derivative variables. The left side is
$N_A^*=K_A(a-x)/x$, while the two growth terms are
$F_A(N_A^*)=K_Aa(a-x)$ and
$F_B(N_B^*)=K_Bb(b-y)$. Finally, on the stationarity curve we have
$y=y_A(x)$. Substituting these four expressions into the patch $A$
equilibrium equation gives the scalar equation
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:no-take-a-scalar-root}
    K_A\frac{a-x}{x}
    =
    (1-m)K_Aa(a-x)+mK_Bb\bigl(b-y_A(x)\bigr).
\end{equation}
After this equation is solved for $x$, set $y=y_A(x)$. The effort in
patch $B$ is recovered from the patch $B$ equilibrium constraint,
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*=(1-e_B)G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
Solving this constraint for $e_B$ gives
\begin{equation*}
    e_B^*=1-\frac{N_B^*}{G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*)}.
\end{equation*}
Here
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*=K_B\frac{b-y}{y}
\end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*}
    G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*)
    =
    mK_Aa(a-x)+(1-m)K_Bb(b-y).
\end{equation*}
Therefore
\begin{equation*}
    e_B^*
    =
    1-
    \frac{K_B(b-y)/y}
         {mK_Aa(a-x)+(1-m)K_Bb(b-y)}.
\end{equation*}
Together with the effort-recovery formula, the root of
\eqref{eq:no-take-a-scalar-root} gives a no-take-in-$A$ edge candidate
when $0<e_B^*<1$.

The edge $e_B=0$ is symmetric. Solving
\eqref{eq:no-take-stationarity-curve} for $x$ gives
\begin{equation*}
    x_B(y)
    =
    \sqrt{
        \frac{1-(1-m)y^2}
             {1-m+(2m-1)y^2}
    }.
\end{equation*}
Then the protected patch $B$ equilibrium equation becomes
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:no-take-b-scalar-root}
    K_B\frac{b-y}{y}
    =
    mK_Aa\bigl(a-x_B(y)\bigr)+(1-m)K_Bb(b-y).
\end{equation}
After solving \eqref{eq:no-take-b-scalar-root} for $y$, set
$x=x_B(y)$ and recover the effort in patch $A$:
\begin{equation*}
    e_A^*
    =
    1-
    \frac{K_A(a-x)/x}
         {(1-m)K_Aa(a-x)+mK_Bb(b-y)}.
\end{equation*}
Together with the effort-recovery formula, the root of
\eqref{eq:no-take-b-scalar-root} gives the symmetric no-take-in-$B$ edge
candidate when $0<e_A^*<1$.

In the examples below, the scalar equations
\eqref{eq:no-take-a-scalar-root} and \eqref{eq:no-take-b-scalar-root}
are solved numerically using Brent's method. Thus the change of variables
reduces each no-take case to a one-dimensional root-finding problem,
even though it does not produce a short closed-form expression for the
no-take effort.

\subsubsection{Interior Candidates}
We now consider candidates with $0<e_A<1$ and $0<e_B<1$. In the
interior, both effort variables are free to vary, so we use the
Lagrange multiplier method with the two equilibrium constraints. The
Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linked-fishing-yield-lagrangian}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathcal{L}
        &=
        \frac{e_A N_A}{1-e_A}
        +
        \frac{e_B N_B}{1-e_B}\\
        &\quad
        +\lambda_A\left(N_A-(1-e_A)G_A(N_A,N_B)\right)\\
        &\quad
        +\lambda_B\left(N_B-(1-e_B)G_B(N_A,N_B)\right).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
An interior optimum must satisfy
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial N_A}=0,
    \qquad
    \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial N_B}=0,
    \qquad
    \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial e_A}=0,
    \qquad
    \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial e_B}=0,
\end{equation*}
together with the two equilibrium constraints in
\eqref{eq:linked-fishing-yield-constraints}. The equilibrium constraints
select the feasible surface. The derivative equations above then say
that, along this feasible surface, the yield has no first-order increase
in any interior direction.

The effort derivatives simplify first. At equilibrium,
\begin{equation*}
    G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)=\frac{N_A^*}{1-e_A},
    \qquad
    G_B(N_A^*,N_B^*)=\frac{N_B^*}{1-e_B},
\end{equation*}
so the effort equations determine the multipliers. For example, the
terms in $\mathcal{L}$ that depend on $e_A$ are
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{e_A N_A^*}{1-e_A}
    +
    \lambda_A
    \left(N_A^*-(1-e_A)G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)\right).
\end{equation*}
Therefore
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial e_A}
    =
    \frac{N_A^*}{(1-e_A)^2}
    +
    \lambda_A G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*).
\end{equation*}
Using $G_A(N_A^*,N_B^*)=N_A^*/(1-e_A)$ and setting the derivative to
zero gives $\lambda_A=-1/(1-e_A)$. The same calculation for patch $B$
gives
\begin{equation*}
    \lambda_A=-\frac{1}{1-e_A},
    \qquad
    \lambda_B=-\frac{1}{1-e_B}.
\end{equation*}
Substituting these into the population derivatives gives
\begin{align*}
    0
        &=
        \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial N_A}\\
        &=
        \frac{e_A}{1-e_A}
        -\frac{1}{1-e_A}
        \left(1-(1-e_A)(1-m)F_A'(N_A^*)\right)
        +mF_A'(N_A^*)\\
        &=
        -1+\bigl((1-m)+m\bigr)F_A'(N_A^*)\\
        &=
        -1+F_A'(N_A^*).
\end{align*}
Therefore $F_A'(N_A^*)=1$. The same calculation for population $B$
gives $F_B'(N_B^*)=1$. Thus,
\begin{equation*}
    F_A'(N_A^*)=1,
    \qquad
    F_B'(N_B^*)=1.
\end{equation*}
Since
\begin{equation*}
    F_A'(N_A)=\frac{K_A^2r_A}{(K_A+N_A)^2},
    \qquad
    F_B'(N_B)=\frac{K_B^2r_B}{(K_B+N_B)^2},
\end{equation*}
the interior candidate has
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=K_A(\sqrt{r_A}-1),
    \qquad
    N_B^*=K_B(\sqrt{r_B}-1).
\end{equation*}
At this point we know the candidate equilibrium populations. The
corresponding efforts are then determined by the equilibrium constraints.
First evaluate the pre-fishing, post-movement quantities $G_A$ and
$G_B$ at the candidate populations. Since
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        F_A\bigl(K_A(\sqrt{r_A}-1)\bigr)
            &=
            K_A\sqrt{r_A}(\sqrt{r_A}-1),\\
        F_B\bigl(K_B(\sqrt{r_B}-1)\bigr)
            &=
            K_B\sqrt{r_B}(\sqrt{r_B}-1),
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
we define
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        G_A^*
            &=
            (1-m)K_A\sqrt{r_A}(\sqrt{r_A}-1)
            +mK_B\sqrt{r_B}(\sqrt{r_B}-1),\\
        G_B^*
            &=
            mK_A\sqrt{r_A}(\sqrt{r_A}-1)
            +(1-m)K_B\sqrt{r_B}(\sqrt{r_B}-1).
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Here $G_A^*$ is the amount present in patch $A$ after growth and
movement but before fishing, and $G_B^*$ is the corresponding amount in
patch $B$. The equilibrium constraints require
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=(1-e_A^*)G_A^*,
    \qquad
    N_B^*=(1-e_B^*)G_B^*.
\end{equation*}
Solving these two equations for the efforts gives
\begin{equation*}
    e_A^*=1-\frac{K_A(\sqrt{r_A}-1)}{G_A^*},
    \qquad
    e_B^*=1-\frac{K_B(\sqrt{r_B}-1)}{G_B^*}.
\end{equation*}
This is an interior candidate only when the computed efforts satisfy
$0<e_A^*,e_B^*<1$. When this holds, the candidate must be compared with
the boundary candidates above. The extinction boundary does not give a
positive yield maximum, because at extinction the equilibrium
population, and therefore the equilibrium yield, is zero.

\subsubsection{Protected Source and Harvested Sink}
One interesting endpoint is the strategy of closing fishing in one patch
and harvesting the other patch as much as possible. In this situation,
the protected patch acts as a source population, while movement carries
part of its production into the harvested patch. The three candidate
families above recover this case naturally.

\noindent\textbf{Case $(e_A,e_B)=(0,1)$: protect $A$, harvest $B$.}
On the edge $e_B=1$, the complete-harvest-in-$B$ candidate is
\begin{equation*}
    e_A^*=1-\frac{1}{(1-m)\sqrt{r_A}}.
\end{equation*}
Therefore the candidate lands at the corner $e_A=0$, $e_B=1$ exactly
when
\begin{equation*}
    (1-m)\sqrt{r_A}=1.
\end{equation*}
Thus the maximum can occur at the policy
\begin{equation*}
    e_A=0,
    \qquad
    e_B=1,
\end{equation*}
meaning no fishing in patch $A$ and complete harvesting in patch $B$. At
this endpoint,
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*=0,
    \qquad
    N_A^*=K_A\left(r_A(1-m)-1\right).
\end{equation*}
Under the condition $(1-m)\sqrt{r_A}=1$, this becomes
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=K_A(\sqrt{r_A}-1),
    \qquad
    Y=K_A(\sqrt{r_A}-1)^2.
\end{equation*}
For example, with $K_A=K_B=1$, $r_A=4$, $r_B=0.8$, and $m=1/2$, the
condition holds and the endpoint candidate is
\begin{equation*}
    (e_A^*,e_B^*)=(0,1),
    \qquad
    (N_A^*,N_B^*)=(1,0),
    \qquad
    Y=1.
\end{equation*}

\noindent\textbf{Case $(e_A,e_B)=(1,0)$: protect $B$, harvest $A$.}
The symmetric endpoint starts from the edge $e_A=1$. The
complete-harvest-in-$A$ candidate is
\begin{equation*}
    e_B^*=1-\frac{1}{(1-m)\sqrt{r_B}},
\end{equation*}
so it lands at $e_A=1$, $e_B=0$ when
\begin{equation*}
    (1-m)\sqrt{r_B}=1.
\end{equation*}
At this endpoint,
\begin{equation*}
    N_A^*=0,
    \qquad
    N_B^*=K_B\left(r_B(1-m)-1\right).
\end{equation*}
Under the condition $(1-m)\sqrt{r_B}=1$, this becomes
\begin{equation*}
    N_B^*=K_B(\sqrt{r_B}-1),
    \qquad
    Y=K_B(\sqrt{r_B}-1)^2.
\end{equation*}
For $m=0.3$, this gives
\begin{equation*}
    r_B=\frac{1}{(1-0.3)^2}\approx2.0408.
\end{equation*}
With $K_A=K_B=1$ and $r_A=0.8$, the endpoint candidate is
\begin{equation*}
    (e_A^*,e_B^*)=(1,0),
    \qquad
    (N_A^*,N_B^*)\approx(0,0.4286),
    \qquad
    Y\approx0.1837.
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Candidate Summary}
After the extinction criterion has been checked, the preceding cases
give a finite list of positive-yield candidate maximizers. The global
maximum is found by evaluating every valid candidate and choosing the
one with largest yield. A candidate is valid only when all formulas are
real and computable, the recovered populations are nonnegative, and the
computed efforts lie in the unit square $0\leq e_A,e_B\leq1$, including
the boundary required by that case.

For the no-take candidates, the scalar equations do not directly give
the populations. The procedure is: solve
\eqref{eq:no-take-a-scalar-root} or \eqref{eq:no-take-b-scalar-root}
for the derivative variable, recover the other derivative variable from
\eqref{eq:no-take-stationarity-curve}, and then recover
$N_A^*$ and $N_B^*$ from \eqref{eq:no-take-population-recovery}.

\subsubsection{Possible Extension: Optimizing Connectivity}
In this paper the movement parameter $m$ is treated as fixed. In some
real-world settings, however, management can also influence connectivity:
for example, by opening or closing migration corridors, changing habitat
quality between regions, or modifying barriers that affect movement.
Then the yield maximization problem would have three decision variables,
\begin{equation*}
    e_A,\qquad e_B,\qquad m.
\end{equation*}
The same general method could be applied: write the equilibrium
constraints, form the constrained yield problem, and compare interior and
boundary candidates. This extension is outside the scope of the present
paper, where the goal is to understand the effect of fishing efforts for
a fixed linked system.
